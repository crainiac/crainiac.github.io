User authentication is an arms race. Donovan had been in it for a while. Predictably, it had escalated to the point where you couldn't really rely on anything besides biometrics. But then it got worse.

A face, fingerprint, or retina is easy to fake. Obviously. But, more interestingly, so is an entire human. Think about it.

What makes you unique? Like, verifiably? You might say that it's your DNA, to which Donovan would reply with some rhetorical questions about from which tissues and at what time and resolution you'd source that DNA for user authentication. He'd had these conversations six jazillion times, to the point where he now knew the definition of "perdurantism" by heart and had coined a past contract Project Theseus.

But Donovan had a bigger problem, which was that vampires had taken over the government of the world. He hadn't known that when he'd started this job, of course, but the subliminals were obvious in hindsight, like the time they'd accidentally CC'd him on an e-mail which referred to him as Count Authula. The call for proposals to red team a blood-based authentication protocol had been pretty on-the-nose, now that he thought about it. Fuck.

Anyways, now Donovan's on a video call with some of these vampire fucks - or their AI avatars? Hard to tell - and they're expressing chilled displeasure at his rate of progress on pheno2hemo. Don's basically at the point where he'd rather they rip him to shreds than drone on ("Bring it, pussies!" he imagines himself screaming, his last words), but he's not doing this for him. There's no hope of saving his family - they've already murdered his wife and daughter - and yet still they can leverage the prospect of harming their reanimated puppet-bodies to render their mastery over him the lesser suffering. Shit, maybe he is doing it for him.

"Did you consider using a GFM?" says Lana, her background a beautiful animated beach. In real life, that sunlight would sublimate her; even vampires want what they can't have. Actually, it might be their defining trait. Lana's question is idiotic: Don has been using genomic foundation models, and routinely updating this circle about it, for the past seven months.

"Great question," he says. "I've done some research on it, but I should look into it more."

"Yes," says Bruce, "and do not forget to check latest FuckMeet GFM; Spencer tells me it is very good."

"I have heard from Spencer also that," Lana agrees.

"Now, Donovan," says Nav, who likes to be the KPI guy, "with this new FuckMeet model, how fast we will accelerate reliability progress?"

Don knows better than to say that it's hard to say; perhaps they'll torture him if he doesn't do the impossible, but they'll definitely do so if he doesn't sound convinced of its doability. The problem is that FuckMeet F8 isn't a GFM at all: it was optimized end-to-end to predict an orgy of users' offsprings' enduring traits, a very different task from reconstructing the gustatory properties of a person's blood at the moment of consumption given only video data of that person being consumed. It's such a subjective problem, honestly. Don is worried that they are already near Bayes error for pheno2hemo, which would dramatically update his P(survival).

"I think that FuckMeet is exactly what we need," he says, slowly, buying himself five more seconds to think. "As for accelerating, I think the best thing we could do is scale up our compute, to fully harness the power of, uh, FuckMe-"

Bruce holds up a hushing hand. "How much?"

Fuck it. Now or never.

"100X."

"That is lot," says Lana.

"That'll get us that second nine," Don lies, "in ninety days. It would probably take about a quarter-century otherwise. That's just the math. It's doable, but our speed is bottlenecked by compute."

Each person has its own terroir. Profoundly distinct, really. In his one hundred seventy-seven years of life, Nav has consumed about twenty-five thousand of them; at 99% reliability, over two thousand of those which have heretofore eluded his revisitation could be tasted once more. His tongue laps his lower lip a little, like the upper one's hooked.

"Ninety to nine," he says. "Sounds nice."

"We can do this," says Bruce, "but, Donovan, you know what happens if you do not do this."

"I will do this."

It won't be enough to get them what they want. But it might be enough to kill them all.
